Chapter 7: Conclusion

This dissertation started with a simple question: can watching how users behave on a phishing style page tell us something that static tools miss? URL checks, certificates, content similarity: they all run before or at page load. Once a user is on the page, clicking and typing and scrolling, most antiphishing tools go quiet. I wanted to know whether that silence was a gap worth filling. PhishShield AI was built to explore that. This chapter looks back at what that exploration achieved, what it means, and where it could go next.

7.1 Answering the Research Question

Chapter 1 posed the research question: Can a lightweight behaviour based monitoring system detect phishing relevant interaction patterns more effectively than static preload analysis alone?

I can't claim "more effectively" with numbers. I didn't run a head to head experiment against static tools. What I can claim is that behavioural monitoring is feasible and that it adds a dimension static analysis cannot. The prototype captures clicks, keypresses, scroll depth, and timing. It turns those into a risk score and a list of factors in plain language. The patterns it flags (very fast first interaction, low scroll with high input activity, high action density) are exactly the kind of thing the literature associates with rushed or credential focused behaviour. Static tools never see that because they are not designed to look at in-session behaviour. So the answer to the question, in the form I can give it, is: yes, behavioural monitoring can surface patterns that static preload analysis alone cannot. Whether it would improve detection in the wild would need a proper study with real users and real phishing scenarios. But the feasibility case is made.

7.2 Revisiting the Gap

In the introduction I talked about a gap in the literature: we don't know much about what happens during a user session on a phishing like page. How quickly do people react? How much do they scroll? Do they type without reading? Most research and tools focus on outcomes: did they click? did they enter credentials? This project focused on the process. I wanted to see if the way people interact, not just what they interact with, could say something useful.

PhishShield AI shows that it can. The prototype records that process: time to first action, scroll depth over time, click and keypress counts. The heuristics I used (fast first interaction, low scroll with typing) come from the literature. They are not perfect and they can produce false positives, but they are interpretable. A user can see why they were flagged, and that matters. If we want to move beyond outcome only measurement, we need systems that can explain themselves, and this one does.

7.3 What the Project Delivered

Looking back at the six objectives from Chapter 1:

Objective 1 (literature review): I completed the literature review. Chapter 2 covered phishing techniques, user interaction research, and behavioural indicators, and it laid the groundwork for the heuristics I used later.

Objective 2 (design for capture): I completed the design. Chapter 4 set out the full architecture, including the simulated phishing page, the backend, and the dashboard, with all capture types specified: clicks, keypresses, scroll depth, and timing.

Objective 3 (implement a functional prototype): I built a working prototype. Chapter 5 described what was created: a single HTML file for the phishing sim, a test events page, and a dashboard. Events flow to the backend, and risk scores and factors are returned in response.

Objective 4 (test with realistic scenarios, assess accuracy, false positive rate, detection latency): I partially met this objective. I tested with realistic interactions such as searching, clicking shortcuts, typing, and scrolling. I could not quantify accuracy or false positive rate because I had no ground truth, so I reframed the evaluation as qualitative validation. Detection latency is effectively zero when the user opens the dashboard. Chapter 6 went through this in detail.

Objective 5 (evaluate usability of risk outputs): I partially met this objective. I designed the outputs for clarity and received positive informal feedback, but I did not run a formal usability study. That was another trade off I had to accept.

Objective 6 (document limitations and future improvements): I completed this. The limitations are discussed in Chapter 6, and the future work section appears below.

So four objectives were fully met and two were partially met. The partially met ones are the ones that assumed resources I did not have, and I am okay with that. The project still shows what it set out to show: that behavioural monitoring of phishing like interactions is feasible and can produce meaningful, interpretable feedback.

7.4 Contribution

What does this dissertation add? A working prototype that bridges static preload checks and behavioural observation. Most antiphishing work stays on one side or the other, but PhishShield AI sits in the middle. It does not replace URL analysis or certificate checks; it is meant to complement them by asking a different question: once the user is on the page, what are they doing? Researchers have not paid much attention to that question before, and this project is an attempt to address it.

The contribution is modest in scope. It was built by a single person, with one prototype, in a simulated environment, using rule based heuristics and no formal user study. Even so, it is concrete. The code exists, the system runs, and the heuristics behave as intended. Someone could take this and extend it by adding ML, more scenarios, or a proper study.

7.5 Limitations in Context

I have been upfront about the limitations throughout. The following subsections detail each limitation, in line with the structure of A* dissertations where limitations are explicitly enumerated.

7.5.1 No Formal User Study

I tested the system myself and showed it to a few informal viewers. Real users might behave differently. A formal study with recruited participants would provide stronger evidence.

7.5.2 No Ground Truth

There was no labelled dataset. No precision, recall, or F1 score could be computed. The evaluation is qualitative rather than quantitative. Accuracy and false positive rate could not be quantified.

7.5.3 No Comparative Trial

I did not run a head to head experiment against static phishing detection tools. The claim that behavioural monitoring adds a dimension is based on feasibility and literature, not on comparative empirical results.

7.5.4 Simulated Environment

The phishing page mimics search and login flows. I did not test other scenarios such as fake notifications, invoice scams, or urgent messages. Generalisability to broader phishing contexts is unknown.

7.5.5 Backend Dependency

The system requires a running backend. If the backend is down, events are not stored. There is no offline capture or deferred sync for real world robustness.

7.5.6 Testing Coverage

There were no automated unit or integration tests. Testing was manual and Chrome only. No performance testing, load testing, or accessibility audit was conducted.

Despite these limits, I believe the project still says something useful. Showing that something can be done is still a result. It is different from showing that it should be done or that it outperforms alternatives. This dissertation does the first; the second and third would need more work. But you have to start somewhere, and this is where I started.

7.6 Future Work

If someone (or I) were to continue this, the natural next steps would be:

A formal user study would be the most obvious next step. You would recruit participants, run benign and phishing like tasks, and label the data. From there you could compute precision, recall, and false positive rate, and compare behavioural monitoring against static tools. That would turn feasibility into evidence.

Machine learning is another direction. The heuristics are simple, and an ML model trained on labelled behavioural data might discriminate better. The architecture leaves room for it: the backend could host a model, and the dashboard would simply display the output. The interpretability challenge would remain, because ML is harder to explain than rules, but it is still a direction worth pursuing.

Broader phishing scenarios would strengthen the case. The simulation mimics search and login, but real phishing includes fake notifications, invoice scams, and urgent messages. Testing whether the heuristics generalise to those contexts would tell us more.

Offline support would make the system more robust. Right now the client needs a running backend; adding local storage and deferred sync could help in real world use.

Integration with existing tools is also possible. PhishShield AI could sit alongside browser antiphishing features, with static checks at load time and behavioural analysis during the session. The two together might be stronger than either alone.

7.7 Personal Reflection

I started this project wanting to build something that could help. Phishing is a real problem and people fall for it. Static tools catch a lot, but not everything. The gap I saw was behavioural: we do not look at what happens during a session. I wanted to try.

The result is a prototype that works. It captures behaviour, produces risk feedback, and explains why it flagged someone. It is not production ready and it is not validated against real users, but it is proof that the idea is viable. Sometimes that is enough. You prove the concept and someone else, or you later, takes it further.

I had to let go of some ambitions along the way, including accuracy numbers, false positive rates, and a formal usability study. I reframed instead of pretending, and I think that is the right way to do it. A dissertation can be honest about what it did not achieve and still be worthwhile. This one is.

7.8 Closing Remarks

Where does this leave things? The question I started with was worth asking, and I have given it a first answer. The rest is for others to build on or challenge.

Phishing will not go away. Attackers adapt and tools adapt with them. This project is one small piece of that ongoing conversation. It does not solve the problem, but it opens a direction. If that direction turns out to matter, the work here will have been a useful starting point. If it does not, the question was still worth asking.
